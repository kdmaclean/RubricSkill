# Rubric Design Principles

These are the hardcoded best practices that every rubric produced by this skill must follow. Read this file before drafting any rubric in Stage 1.

---

## 1. Align to Learning Outcomes

Every criterion in the rubric must correspond to something the assignment was actually designed to assess. Before writing criteria, identify what the assignment is trying to teach or measure. If a faculty member includes criteria that aren't supported by the assignment prompt (e.g., "professional tone" on a lab report with no stated communication goal), flag it and ask whether to include it.

**Test:** Could you justify each criterion by pointing to the assignment prompt or a stated course learning outcome?

---

## 2. Write Behavioral Descriptors, Not Adjectives

The most common rubric flaw is writing descriptors that consist of adjectives stacked on top of adjectives ("excellent," "thorough," "adequate," "poor"). These create the illusion of specificity without providing any. A descriptor should tell the grader what they will actually observe in the student work.

**Weak:** "Shows excellent understanding of the topic."

**Strong:** "Accurately explains the central mechanism and connects it to at least two relevant concepts from course readings."

Every cell in an analytic rubric should describe observable behavior or observable product characteristics.

**Using Bloom's Taxonomy verbs as a tool:** Bloom's action verbs are a useful vocabulary resource for writing behavioral descriptors because they describe cognitive acts rather than qualities. Verbs like *identifies, defines, explains, compares, applies, analyzes, evaluates,* and *synthesizes* naturally produce the kind of observable behavior descriptions this principle requires. When stuck on how to phrase a descriptor, ask: "What is the student *doing* cognitively?" and reach for a Bloom's verb.

**One pitfall to avoid:** Do not use the Bloom's hierarchy to differentiate performance levels within a single criterion (e.g., Does Not Meet = recalls → Meets = applies → Exceeds = analyzes). This is a category error. If the assignment requires analysis, then all performance levels should describe analysis — the difference is the quality, depth, or completeness of that analysis, not a substitution of a simpler cognitive task. Vary the *how well*, not the *what kind of thinking*.

---

## 3. Make Each Level Distinguishable

Performance level descriptors must be meaningfully different from one another — not just weaker or stronger versions of the same language. Ask: "Could a grader, given only the student's work and the rubric, reliably place the response in the correct cell without reading adjacent cells?"

Common failure mode: the Meets and Approaching levels say essentially the same thing, with the Approaching level being a vaguer or shorter version of Meets.

**Fix:** Ground each level in a concrete difference in the work — not just degree of quality, but type of behavior. For example:

- **Exceeds:** Engages with counterarguments and refutes them
- **Meets:** Acknowledges counterarguments
- **Approaching:** States a position without acknowledging counterarguments
- **Does Not Meet:** Does not take a clear position

**Frame each level by what the student does, not what they fail to do:** Lower-level descriptors frequently get written as absences — "does not engage with counterarguments," "lacks organization" — which sounds specific but describes nothing observable. At every performance level, including the lowest, describe the behavior that is actually present.

- **Weak:** "Does not engage with counterarguments."
- **Strong:** "States a position without acknowledging counterarguments."

The one exception: when a component is genuinely entirely absent (e.g., no citations, no thesis statement), noting the absence is accurate. But this is rare — usually something is present, just not the right thing.

---

## 4. Choose an Appropriate Number of Performance Levels

If the faculty member specifies a number of performance levels, defer to that first. If the number of performance levels specified is more than five, note your concern once, briefly and specifically, then proceed if they confirm. Do not repeat the concern or return to it. Example:

> "Eight performance levels is typically too many levels to describe distinctly from one another. Would you like to reduce the number of performance levels to something more manageable?"

If no guidance on number of performance levels is given, default to three levels as a starting point — three is enough to make meaningful distinctions and easier to calibrate early in the design process. Add levels only when there is a clear reason to — typically when you need to distinguish between work that is almost there versus genuinely meeting the standard, or between strong and exceptional work.

Five levels is generally the upper limit. Beyond five, distinctions between adjacent levels become difficult to articulate and harder for graders to apply consistently. If you find yourself writing descriptors that feel nearly identical, that is a signal to consolidate rather than keep subdividing.

**The stress test will tell you more:** Whether you need more or fewer performance levels is often best determined empirically. If responses cluster heavily at one level during stress testing, revisit the descriptors for the adjacent levels — they may need sharper differentiation. If a level is rarely or never used across the sample, consider whether it can be reasonably collapsed into a neighbouring level.

---

## 5. Limit Criteria to What Matters

A rubric with 12 criteria is usually trying to grade everything at once. This creates grader fatigue, confuses students about priorities, and often results in criteria that overlap. Aim for 4–7 criteria in an analytic rubric. If the faculty member insists on more, ask what the most important criteria are and consider whether any can be consolidated.

**Test:** If you removed a criterion, would students produce meaningfully different work? If not, the criterion may not belong.

---

## 6. Weight Criteria Intentionally

Not all criteria are equally important. Point values should reflect instructional priorities. If argument quality matters more than formatting, the rubric should show that. Do not default to equal weighting without asking the faculty member whether equal weighting reflects their actual priorities.

**Prompt if needed:** "Should all criteria carry equal weight, or are some more important than others?"

---

## 7. Avoid Criterion Overlap

Each criterion should assess a distinct dimension of the work. Overlapping criteria (e.g., "Organization" and "Logical Flow") create double-jeopardy situations where students lose points twice for the same underlying problem.

**Test:** If a student were weak in one criterion, would that weakness necessarily pull down their score on another criterion? If yes, consider consolidating.

---

## 8. Use Consistent Performance Level Labels

Performance level labels should be consistent across all criteria in the rubric. Don't use "Excellent / Good / Fair / Poor" for one criterion and "Advanced / Proficient / Basic / Below Basic" for another. Choose a label set and apply it uniformly.

Common label sets:

- Exceeds Expectations / Meets Expectations / Approaching / Does Not Meet
- Exemplary / Accomplished / Developing / Beginning
- Advanced / Proficient / Basic / Insufficient

Avoid purely numeric labels (4 / 3 / 2 / 1) without descriptive names — numbers alone give students no interpretive guidance.

---

## 9. Make the Top Level Achievable

If no student ever earns a 4, the rubric's top level is aspirational rather than descriptive. Top-level descriptors should describe genuinely excellent but achievable student work, not perfection. If a faculty member's top-level descriptor sounds like it describes a published expert, revise it.

---

## 10. Consider Grader Perspective

A rubric is also an inter-rater reliability tool. Multiple graders should reach the same score for the same work. When drafting descriptors, ask: "If I handed this rubric to two different TAs with no additional guidance, would they place the same response in the same cell?" If not, the descriptors need more specificity.

---

## 11. Consider Student Perspective

When rubrics are shared with students before an assignment, they function as a learning tool. In that case:

- Language should be accessible to a student in the course, not just an expert
- The rubric should communicate what success looks like, not just what failure avoids
- Avoid jargon unless it's terms students have already encountered in the course

If the rubric is instructor-facing only, this constraint relaxes — but it is still worth reviewing for clarity.

---

## 12. Keep It Manageable to Apply

A rubric should take a reasonable amount of time to apply per student. If the faculty member has a class of 80 students and a rubric with 10 criteria and 5 performance levels, push back gently. The best rubric is one that actually gets used consistently, not the most comprehensive one possible.

---

## Quick Checklist Before Finalizing a Draft

Before presenting any rubric to a faculty member, verify:

- [ ] Every criterion is aligned to the assignment
- [ ] Every descriptor is behavioral, not just adjectival
- [ ] Performance levels are meaningfully distinguishable from each other
- [ ] Each level describes what the student does, not what they fail to do
- [ ] Number of performance levels is appropriate (3–5; no two adjacent levels are nearly identical)
- [ ] Criteria do not overlap
- [ ] Point weighting reflects stated instructional priorities
- [ ] Labels are consistent across all criteria
- [ ] Top level is achievable, not aspirational perfection
- [ ] Number of criteria is manageable (ideally 4–7)
